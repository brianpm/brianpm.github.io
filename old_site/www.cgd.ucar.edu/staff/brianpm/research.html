RESEARCH

Overview

I am interested in the role that clouds play in the climate system. Clouds embody complicated, delicate interactions of atmospheric dynamics and thermodynamics, which come together to substantially alter the state of the atmosphere. Because so many scales are involved, from sub-millimeter (e.g. nucleation) to thousands of kilometers (e.g. planetary-scale waves), from seconds to years, the representation of clouds in climate models is challenging, and has remained a source of uncertainty in projections of future climate. My work uses these models to try to (1) better understand how clouds interact with the climate system and (2) to improve the representation of clouds in climate models.

One of the fundamental questions I want to help answer is simply, In a changing climate, how will clouds change? It's a simple question, but as yet we do not have an answer.

Some general interests that are related include: moist convection in various regimes (e.g., stratocumulus, shallow convection, and deep, precipitating convection), surface-atmosphere interaction, boundary layer processes, the hydrologic cycle, climate feedbacks (& climate sensitivity/stability), and climate modeling.

Themes

  • Low-level clouds.

    • Low clouds remain at the heart of "uncertainty" in estimates of climate sensitivity. Essentially all of my research is centered on the role of low-level clouds connected to the boundary layer in the climate system. I'm cheifly interested in tropical clouds, like subtropical stratocumulus decks and the ubiquitous shallow cumulus that cover the oceans, but I am also interested in their land-based and extratropical relatives.

  • Boundary layers and turbulence.

    • The low-clouds that I am interested in are connected to turbulent boundary layers. To understand the clouds requires understanding the structure of the boundary layer. Boundary layers come in many forms, and play an important part of our everyday interaction with the weather, which includes impacts on air quality.

  • Isolating regime behavior.

    • Many studies are published that seem to expect climate models to reproduce observations precisely. The reality is that all climate models have biases, some severe, which can substantially change when and where particular phenomena occur. Rather than focusing on specific time intervals or pre-determined geographical regions, I like to explore the physics of the climate models by isolating meteorological conditions in which my targeted phenomena occur (e.g., stratocumulus decks). A prominent example in my work is using large-scale conditions to isolate different low-cloud regimes ([LINK], [LINK]), but I apply similar thinking in a study of the strength of the Arctic inversion strength [LINK].

  • Idealized modeling frameworks.

    • My focus has been on trying to understand climate phenomena in idealized GCM configurations. This goes back to my work with the UCLA GCM with constant sunshine [LINK], which also employed a simple bulk boundary layer model, but continues to my current projects using GCMs in aquaplanet configurations and with a short-term forecast approach.

  • Model evaluation with observations.

    • Examples include our work to show that simplified aquaplanets can be directly compared with observations [LINK]. This is also related to my work with the space-based lidar on CALIPSO [LINK], and my contribution to ongoing work with satellite simulators in CAM [LINK].

Projects

☁ Evaluating climate models using a short-term forecast framework (known as CAPT or Transpose AMIP). By specifying a realistic atmospheric state, we investigate how fast processes drive a model away from that state and toward the model's preferred state. This method has been shown especially useful for examining cloud-associated processes ("CA" in CAPT).
